A Biography

Mick Mars had been in several bands in the 1970s, including one called Mottley Croo. He got his stage name from a bandmate, Michelle Meyers, who had the stage name "Micki Marz" in 1977. He legally changed his name to Mick Mars when he moved from the midwest to Hollywood prior to joining Mötley Crüe. Mick Mars (born Robert Alan Deal; either May 4, 1951 or April 4, 1955) is an American musician and one of kind guitarist. He is the guitarist for the heavy metal band Mötley Crüe, the band retired in 2015.

Since the early 1980s, Mötley Crue has defined the Los Angeles metal scene. Powered by the Guitars of Mick Mars, the group dominated the rock charts throughout the ’80s, producing a stream of albums and hits that have impacted the music world and people through the decades. Crüe hammered through the grunge era, and during that time, frontman Vince Neil and drummer Tommy Lee left for personal reasons before finding their way back to re-join Mick Mars and bassist Nikki Sixx.

1983's Shout at the Devil, put these Hollywood bad boys on the metal map as Mars and the band dominated '80s metal. They released three multi-platinum albums in a row; 1985's Theatre of Pain, 1987's Girls, Girls, Girls, and their most commercially successful album to date, 1989's Dr. Feelgood, the end result of Mars' catchy rock riffs.

Mars' primary stage guitar was a Fender Stratocaster with an "HSH" (humbucker, single coil, humbucker) configuration. He frequently used a Stratocaster modified from components of 1963, 1964, and 1965 models turning it in to a personaly modified Strat. Mick Mars stated "I look for something I can play. I don’t buy pristine guitars, because I like to play them. If I drop them, I don’t care. So I’m a collector of “player” guitars – those I’m not afraid to pick up and bang on. I typically don’t buy guitars for looks; I buy them for tone – the sound, the output, and that kind of thing is most important. I’m a tone freak".

Mick Mars has been fighting health issues since he was 17 years of age. He has been diagnosed with ankylosing spondylitis. Mick Mars had a minimal problem to start with but the pain and immobilization of his spine increased gradually over time.

Mick Mars had called it an inconvenience in one of the interviews a few years back. But gradually the pain and freezing of his lower back had led to a lot of other problems. He was operated on for hip problems and a total hip replacement was performed on him in 2004.
